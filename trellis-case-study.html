Designing an AI Bot
to Guide How L&D Works

How I designed Trellis — an AI-powered instructional design assistant — and discovered that the real power of AI comes from teams building together, not in silos.

01 · Situation

Hand-selected for an org-wide AI initiative — with no playbook to follow.

Studio 22 was a small, selective internal working group at Inteleos — fewer than 10 people across the organization — tasked with building AI tools to solve real operational problems. I was selected as one of the L&D representatives.

The challenge I focused on: the L&D team's certificate program development process was complex, cross-functional, and compliance-heavy. It required constant coordination across Instructional Design, Compliance, Product, and SMEs — and there was no shared intelligence layer to guide the work, catch gaps early, or keep everyone aligned.

My question: What if an AI bot could act as an embedded instructional design consultant — guiding the process, surfacing gaps, and keeping teams moving forward?

02 · What I Built

Trellis — an AI bot that thinks like an instructional designer.

Trellis is a structured AI assistant built to guide the full lifecycle of certificate program development — from needs analysis through ANAB accreditation documentation. It's not a generic chatbot. It operates with phase-awareness, institutional knowledge, and a defined set of behaviors designed specifically for the L&D context at Inteleos.

🗺 Phase-Based Guidance

Tracks where a project is in the development lifecycle and provides context-appropriate support at each stage.

📋 11-Template System

Provisions the right template for the right moment — from JTA frameworks to assessment blueprints to ANAB documentation.

⚠️ Gap Identification

Proactively flags missing information, names which department can provide it, and explains the timeline impact.

✅ Readiness Checks

Assesses whether a project is truly ready to move to the next phase — not just whether it feels ready.

🔗 Compliance Integration

Built-in awareness of ANAB E2659 and 17024 standards — surfaces documentation requirements before they become blockers.

🤝 Cross-Functional Handoffs

Generates handoff checklists and clarifies what each team (L&D, Compliance, Product) needs to contribute at each stage.

Trellis provides structure, not content — it guides the process, defers to clinical SMEs on expertise, and escalates compliance questions to the people who own them. It was designed to augment human judgment, not replace it.

03 · How I Designed It

Instructional design applied to AI behavior.

Building Trellis required the same thinking I apply to any learning design problem: who is the user, what do they need at each stage, where do they typically get stuck, and what does "good" look like at every step?

  • 1

    Mapped the full program development workflow — identified every phase from needs analysis through launch, and documented what decisions, templates, and stakeholders are involved at each stage.

  • 2

    Defined the bot's persona and boundaries — Trellis has a clear identity, a defined scope, and graceful handoff language for moments where it should defer to human expertise.

  • 3

    Built a 11-template provisioning system — each template is tied to a phase and purpose, so Trellis delivers the right tool at the right moment rather than overwhelming users with options.

  • 4

    Designed contextual triggers — Trellis activates specific behaviors based on what the user asks, including timeline generation, Asana project setup, and compliance gap flagging.

  • 5

    Built quality self-checks into the system — before providing timelines, templates, or gap flags, Trellis runs internal checks to ensure its output is grounded and appropriate.

  • 6

    Deployed to the L&D team and iterated — Trellis v1.0 launched January 2026 for 3 ETPMs and is actively in use for cross-functional certificate program development.

✦ The Bigger Takeaway

Siloed bots solve siloed problems. The real unlock came from recognizing that when teams build AI tools together — across departments, not just within them — the outcomes are exponentially more powerful. That's not just a lesson about AI. It's a lesson about how organizations learn.

04 · Outcomes

A working tool, a strategic insight, and a model for what's next.

Live Trellis v1.0 actively in use by the L&D team
11 Templates provisioned across the full program development lifecycle
Cross-
org Insight — collaborative AI builds outperform siloed ones

Trellis demonstrated that AI in L&D isn't about replacing instructional designers — it's about giving them a system that holds the complexity of the process so they can focus on the work that requires human judgment. And the Studio 22 experience surfaced something even more valuable: a vision for how organizations should approach AI development — together, not in isolation.

The most powerful thing Studio 22 taught me isn't how to build a bot. It's that when people stop working in silos and start building AI together, something much bigger becomes possible.
