Building the Operations System
Behind the Content

How I designed a workflow and data infrastructure from scratch that gave a credentialing team real-time visibility into item quality, timelines, and bottlenecks.

Program MOC Content Development
Tool Asana
Scope ARDMS & APCA Specialties
Impact Team-wide + Cross-org Adoption

01 · Situation

No dashboard. No visibility. No system.

The MOC (Maintenance of Certification) content development process was running on manual effort — tracking item quality and timelines meant digging into the item bank by hand, every single time someone had a question. There was no centralized place to see where things stood, where things were stuck, or whether OKRs were on track.

For a team responsible for delivering high-quality assessment items across multiple specialties, this wasn't sustainable. It meant answering the same questions repeatedly, losing time to administrative overhead, and having no data to drive process improvement.

My role: sole designer and builder of the entire project management infrastructure — from field architecture to automation to team adoption.

02 · Before & After

What changed when the system existed.

Before
  • Manual item counts from the bank on request
  • No visibility into review stage or bottlenecks
  • OKR tracking was inconsistent and time-consuming
  • SME training gaps had no data backing
  • No repeatable workflow for item development
After
  • Real-time dashboard showing item status at a glance
  • Clear bottleneck identification at each pipeline stage
  • OKR data collected automatically as work progressed
  • Quality patterns surfaced to inform SME training
  • Templated, duplicatable workflow for each specialty

03 · Approach

Built for the team, not just for the data.

The challenge with Asana isn't technical — it's architectural. The tool is only as useful as the thinking behind it. I approached this as a systems design problem: what does this team actually need to see, and how do we make sure the data gets captured without adding burden?

  • 1

    Mapped the existing workflow — identified every stage of the item development process, from initial SME writing through review, revision, and final approval.

  • 2

    Designed the field architecture — determined which data points were essential for quality tracking, timeline reporting, and OKR visibility. Avoided over-engineering.

  • 3

    Built the project dashboard and templates — created reusable project templates for each specialty (ARDMS and APCA) so new cycles could be spun up without rebuilding from scratch.

  • 4

    Automated where possible — reduced manual data entry by setting up rules and triggers within Asana's limitations to move tasks and flag status changes automatically.

  • 5

    Led team adoption — trained the MOC team, CCS staff, and SMEs on the system and iterated based on their feedback to keep it usable, not just functional.

My manager and director were bought in immediately — not because it was impressive, but because it answered questions they'd been asking for years.

04 · Outcomes

Still running. Still growing. Still spreading.

Live System still in active use across the MOC team
2+ Other teams beginning to adopt the same framework
Zero Manual item counts — data is now always accessible

Beyond the tool itself: this project demonstrated that operations infrastructure is a form of educational leadership. When content teams can see their work clearly, they make better decisions about item quality, SME development, and resource allocation. The system didn't just save time — it made the team smarter.
