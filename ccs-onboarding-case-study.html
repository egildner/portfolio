Designing an Onboarding Program
Without a Roadmap

How I built a structured, scalable onboarding experience for a specialized clinical role — without insider access, without prior documentation, and without a playbook to follow.

Program CCS Onboarding
Audience Clinical Content Specialists
Format eLearning + Docs + Video
Tool Asana (Templated)

01 · Situation

A specialized role. No onboarding plan. No one to ask.

Clinical Content Specialists (CCS) are a highly specialized hire — they sit at the intersection of clinical knowledge and assessment development. When a new CCS joins, there's a steep learning curve: understanding the organization, mastering the content development process, and getting up to speed on standards and tools that most people outside the team have never touched.

The problem: there was no formal onboarding program. No structured first week. No clear sequence of what to learn, when, or how. And because the team operates in silos, there was limited ability to pull in existing CCS staff to fill the gaps informally.

My role: sole designer and builder — responsible for scoping, structuring, and producing the entire onboarding program before the first hire went through it.

02 · The Real Design Challenge

Building for a role I wasn't in — in an org that works in silos.

🔒

The core constraint: I wasn't a CCS and couldn't easily consult with those who were. That meant I had to work from first principles — using instructional design expertise, role analysis, and what I could access about the work itself to reverse-engineer what a new hire actually needs to know in week one, month one, and beyond.

This is a common situation in L&D: you're asked to design for a role or audience you can't fully access. The answer isn't to wait for perfect information — it's to build a rigorous structure that holds even when content details are filled in later. That's what I did.

03 · What I Built

A structured, templated program built to scale.

The program was organized into work packages — sequenced learning bundles that balanced flexibility with non-negotiable foundational content. Each package combined multiple content types so new hires could learn in the format that suited the material.

🎓 eLearning Modules Core concepts and standards delivered as structured self-paced content
📄 Reference Documents Job aids, process guides, and role-specific reference materials
🎥 Video Walkthroughs Demonstrations of tools, systems, and workflows in context
  • 1

    Conducted a role and task analysis — mapped what a CCS needs to know, do, and understand to be effective, structured by priority and time-to-competency.

  • 2

    Designed the work package sequence — organized content into a logical week-by-week progression, balancing structured learning with applied practice time.

  • 3

    Built the Asana project template — created a fully templated, duplicatable onboarding project so every new CCS hire gets the same consistent experience with minimal setup effort.

  • 4

    Incorporated time estimates — each work package includes estimated time to complete, giving managers visibility into onboarding load and helping new hires pace themselves.

  • 5

    Designed for future iteration — the template structure makes it easy to update content, add modules, or adapt the program as the role evolves.

The template's time estimates and clear layout made the invisible visible — stakeholders could finally see what onboarding actually takes, not just assume it happens.

04 · Outcomes

A program ready to run — and ready to repeat.

1st Formal onboarding program ever built for this role
∞ Reusable — template duplicates for every new hire
Clear Time visibility for managers and new hires alike

The most important outcome isn't a metric — it's that a new CCS now has a structured, dignified start. They know what to do, in what order, and roughly how long it will take. That clarity reduces anxiety, accelerates ramp time, and signals organizational respect for the role. That's what good onboarding design does.
